Out of the Ashes: A Smarter Way to Map and Respond

Real-World Use Case: Structure-Level Wildfire Damage Assessment Ventura County, CA | Thomas Fire

The Thomas Fire, ignited on December 4, 2017, in Ventura County, California, devastated over 280,000 acres. The blaze destroyed 1,063 structures, including homes, agricultural buildings, and critical facilities such as the Vista del Mar hospital and the Ojai Valley School. The Thomas Fire's widespread destruction underscored the vulnerabilities of both urban and rural communities to large-scale wildfires.

Wildfires like the Thomas Fire leave more than destruction‚Äîthey leave urgent questions. What structures remain? What was lost? How can we respond better next time?

This use case explores how ESRI‚Äôs GeoAI tools automate pre- and post-disaster structure mapping‚Äîspeeding up analysis, reducing manual labor, and delivering accurate, scalable results. Using pre- and post-fire geospatial data, this workflow delivered an actionable structure analysis in days‚Äînot weeks. In response to the 2017 Thomas Fire, we built a flexible, multi-source workflow that integrates:

  • Pre-Fire NAIP Imagery + ESRI‚Äôs Text SAM:
    Extracted building footprints using a simple text prompt ("buildings") to segment structures from 2016 aerial imagery and establish a baseline.
  • Post-Fire LiDAR + Building Point Classification:
    Classified 3D point clouds to isolate surviving buildings and foundations, capturing a highly accurate post-event footprint to assess damage and prioritize mitigation.

This dual-model approach provides a more complete picture of structural damage, critical for FEMA reporting, rebuilding strategies, or hazard mitigation. Our structure-level outputs can help agencies assess damage with spatial accuracy, validate claims, and model risk exposure across large portfolios‚Äîall with AI-driven speed and transparency.

Within the area of interest, the analysis identified 438 of the 1,368 buildings were completely destroyed. The assessed improved value of the destroyed parcels was approximately 106 million dollars.

Deeper Analysis: Post-Fire Landslide Risk

Wildfires increase landslide risk by stripping vegetation and destabilizing slopes. Our workflows combine GeoAI and multi-layer spatial analysis to identify high-risk zones for post-fire debris flows and slope failure. We integrated two data sources from California Geological Survey (CGS):

  • Landslide risk analysis based on slope and rock strength
  • Historical landslide locations

This layered analysis could help emergency managers, infrastructure planners, and insurers proactively address secondary hazards after a wildfire.

Standing structures with the highest landslide risk in Red are those within CGS's 10/10 landslide risk zones & 15 meters from a burnt down structure. Orange structures are within the 10/10 landslide zones but are not near burnt structures. White structures should be analyzed further for vegetation loss since areas where historical landslides occur have a higher chance of reoccurring. Green structures are in relatively safe zones outside all of these factors.
